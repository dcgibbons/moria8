# Moria8 Player Guide

This guide is intentionally more practical than the manual. It is for the first
few hours of play: how to stop dying to avoidable mistakes, how to evaluate
characters, and which systems are easy to underestimate.

## First Town Visit

Before entering the dungeon, buy the boring things:

- Food.
- Light fuel or a spare light source.
- Basic armor if you start fragile.
- A ranged option if your class can use one well.
- Escape or healing consumables when you can afford them.

The General Store solves food and light. The Armory and Weaponsmith solve early
equipment. The Temple, Alchemy Shop, and Magic Shop are where many emergency
items appear. The Black Market can be excellent but is expensive. The Home is
storage, not a normal store.

## Beginner Character Choices

Warrior is the simplest class: strong combat, no magic management. It teaches
movement, corridors, stairs, food, light, and equipment without asking you to
learn spell tempo immediately.

Priest and Paladin are more forgiving than Mage when you want magic and
survivability together. Mage is powerful but fragile. Rogue and Ranger reward
players who use missiles, devices, and utility magic.

Race matters. Half-Troll and Dwarf make sturdier front-line characters.
Halfling, Elf, and Gnome lean more toward searching, devices, and magic play,
but can be less forgiving in direct fights.

## Early Dungeon Rules

Fight in corridors. Open rooms let several monsters reach you at once. If a
room wakes up, back into a passage or leave.

Leave earlier than you think. If you are out of healing, low on light, hungry,
or carrying unknown dangerous items, return to town. Stairs are a tool.

Search dead ends. Secret doors and traps are a major part of Moria. Use `S` for
one search turn or `#` to toggle search mode when you want repeated searching.

Do not run while unsure. `SHIFT` + direction is useful for explored corridors,
but it is not a substitute for paying attention.

## Magic Learning

Use `F` to study when eligible. Use `M` for mage spells and `P` for prayers.
Books matter: carrying the wrong book can mean knowing that magic exists but
being unable to learn or use the next useful effect.

Magic users should protect their prime stat. Intelligence drives mage spell
learning and mana. Wisdom drives priest and paladin prayer learning and mana.

Do not treat every spell as an attack. Detection, light, teleport, curing,
removing curses, identifying items, and creating food often save more lives
than one more damage spell.

Area spells require positioning. Centering yourself before a room-light,
mapping, detection, or area-effect spell can change what the spell reaches.

## Equipment And Identification

Unknown equipment is a bargain only if you can survive the downside. Cursed
items can trap a slot until Remove Curse or equivalent help is available.

Heavy weapons can be bad for weak characters. A huge weapon that you cannot use
well may lower your real survival odds compared with a modest weapon you can
hit with reliably.

Identification is a power curve. Once potions, scrolls, wands, staffs, and
equipment are known, future finds become much safer and faster to use.

## Common Deaths

- Running out of light.
- Running out of food.
- Fighting in open rooms.
- Staying in the dungeon after healing is gone.
- Trying unknown consumables in combat.
- Wearing unidentified gear without a curse plan.
- Ignoring fast monsters.
- Letting casters or dragons keep line of effect.
- Diving deeper because the previous floor felt easy.

## When To Descend

Descend when you can kill common monsters on the current depth without spending
your emergency resources. If every ordinary fight costs a potion, scroll, or
near-death retreat, you are not ready to go deeper.

Better equipment, more levels, known consumables, and reliable escape options
are all valid reasons to return later instead of forcing progress.
